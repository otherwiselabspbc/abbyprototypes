## One-Liner

**Learn to read by learning magic** — an adventure RPG that adapts evidence-based dyslexia tutoring into 3D video game mechanics.

## The Concept

Reading skills are reframed as **magic skills**. Phoneme awareness becomes incantation. Decoding becomes spell-casting. Fluency becomes mastery of the arcane. The learner is a young mage progressing through a magical world, and every reading skill they acquire unlocks new abilities, new rooms, and new regions of that world.

## Tonal and Mechanical Inspirations

| Inspiration | What We Take From It |
|---|---|
| **Harry Potter** | Wand magic, spoken spells, potions, the fantasy of learning magic at school |
| **Breath of the Wild** | Open-world adventure, emergent mechanics, exploration-driven progression |
| **Indiana Jones** | Environmental puzzles, discovery, the thrill of figuring things out |
| **Sky: Children of the Light** | Mobile-first design, expressive world, social warmth, accessible controls |
| **Wario Ware** | Bite-sized mini-games, rapid mechanic variety, playful experimentation |

## Three-Phase Roadmap

### Phase 1 — Mini-Games (Current Phase)

~20 standalone mini-games, each prototyping a novel mechanic that adapts a specific activity from evidence-based dyslexia tutoring (Orton-Gillingham, Lindamood-Bell, Wilson Reading) into a playable game interaction.

Goals:
- Explore the design space rapidly
- Test which mechanics are fun, effective, and retainable
- Build a library of proven interactions
- String games together with a **curriculum backbone** so learners progress through skills in a researched sequence

Think Wario Ware: short, varied, surprising — but each game targets a real skill.

### Phase 2 — Escape Rooms

A closed-world escape room game that sequences the best mini-game mechanics into a structured progression. Each room:
1. **Learn** a new magic/reading skill
2. **Practice** it through guided repetition
3. **Demonstrate mastery** to unlock the next room

### Phase 3 — The Open World

A 3D world inspired by Sky: Children of the Light where the player explores, builds, and uses magic that IS the reading skill — not disconnected mini-games, but the fundamental way the world works.

## Design Principles

- **Evidence-based first.** Every mechanic maps to a real activity from proven dyslexia intervention programs. Fun is the delivery vehicle, not a replacement for rigor.
- **Mobile-first.** Touch, voice, and tilt before keyboard and mouse. Designed for devices learners actually have.
- **Mastery through play.** No worksheets in disguise. If it doesn't feel like a game, it's not ready.
- **Curriculum-sequenced.** Games follow a progression that builds skills systematically, from phonemic awareness through fluency.
- **Magic as metaphor.** The fantasy of learning magic provides intrinsic motivation and makes skill progression feel like power progression.

## Target Audience

Tweens with dyslexia (or at risk), their tutors, and their families. Playable independently or in structured tutoring sessions.

## How to Apply This Skill

**When evaluating a proposed game or mechanic:**
1. Does it map to a real skill from the curriculum? (Check `/curriculum-mapping`)
2. Does it feel like magic — does the reading skill become a magical act?
3. Is it mobile-first? Can it be played with touch alone?
4. Is it self-contained enough for Phase 1, or does it depend on Phase 2/3 infrastructure?
5. Would a tween with dyslexia find it genuinely fun — or just educational?

**Red flags — check with cofounder if:**
- The mechanic requires reading fluency the learner may not yet have
- The magic metaphor feels forced or tacked on
- It would only work with keyboard/mouse
- It spans multiple skill domains (one skill per game — no exceptions in Phase 1)
